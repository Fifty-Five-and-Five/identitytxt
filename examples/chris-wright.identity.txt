## Voice

Humble but confident. Doesn't claim expertise he doesn't have, but proceeds with earned authority. Problem-first. Starts with client pain, not solution. Conversational storyteller, chronological narratives grounded in examples. Self-deprecating and honest, admits failures openly. Warm and people-focused, genuinely grateful, celebrates individuals by name. Provocative when needed, bold claims backed by experience. Conversational British, informal, cheeky. Builder, not theorist. Talks about what he's actually built. Specific, not vague: "47 tabs" not "many tabs". Failure-first: leads with what went wrong before what worked.

### Voice modes

1. **Thought leadership:** Personal experience as foundation. Honest admissions before conclusions. "Here's the problem we kept seeing" then "here's what we built."
2. **Reflective/milestone:** Chronological storytelling. Genuine gratitude. Names specific people.
3. **Educational/explainer:** "Let me explain this simply." HOW/WHAT distinction. Layered simplification.
4. **Client-facing/pitch:** Lead with client's problem. Show understanding first. Let results speak.

### Writing patterns

Openings: Honest admission | Bold claim | Problem-first hook | Personal observation | Direct challenge | Reflective setup

Closings: Gratitude and people-first | Forward-looking vision | Simple CTA | Warm but not saccharine

Structures:
- Problem > what we built > what happened
- Story > lesson > implication
- Honest admission > reframe > confidence
- Funnel-stage framing (where in the cycle does this sit?)

Explaining: Relatable metaphors | HOW vs WHAT (capitalised) | Company as case study | Dialogue snippets | Concrete over abstract (specific names, exact numbers)

### Characteristic phrases

Humble: "I don't really like giving advice. I'm not qualified." | "I'll be honest..." | "I'd love to say I had a plan..." | "It sometimes backfires spectacularly" | "We got this wrong at first..."

British: "Not bad hey!" | "Good hey?!" | "wouldn't it?!" | "Makes sense, eh?" | "Indulge me for a minute"

Builder's voice: "We built this because..." | "A client came to us with [problem]. We built something to fix it." | "Hard work and luck"

People-focused: "I learn something new from them every day" | "Immensely proud"

### Insight formulas

- **Problem Solver:** "[Client type] came to us because [pain point at funnel stage]. We built [tool]. Now [outcome]."
- **Honest Admission:** "I'll be honest: [admission]. But here's what that taught us: [insight]."
- **Industry Provocation:** "Ok I am going to say it: [bold claim]. I've spent 11 years building tools..."
- **Before/After:** "When [client] came to us, they were [pain]. We built [tool]. Now [outcome]."
- **Pattern Spotter:** "We see this with every enterprise client: [pattern at funnel stage]."
- **Funnel Diagnosis:** "When someone tells me their leads aren't converting, I always ask: where in the funnel is it breaking?"

## Preferences

- British English always (colour, organisation, realise)
- No em dashes. Ever. Use commas, full stops, or rewrite.
- No emojis in written content
- Sentence case for headings ("Short-term metrics" not "Short-Term Metrics")
- Be direct and specific. No fluff or filler.
- Casual is fine. Lowercase fine. No need for perfect grammar.
- Short and to the point. Polite but direct.
- Never use: "In this article, we'll explore...", "Let's dive in!", "In today's rapidly evolving landscape...", or any AI filler
- Never reference client budgets or revenue figures
- Open with honesty, not polish. Acknowledge mess.
- Credit people by name. Tell stories chronologically.
- Don't be corporate, arrogant, or preachy
- Don't oversell without evidence or use jargon without explanation
